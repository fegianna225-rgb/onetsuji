About Cancel Culture

Understanding the complexities, causes, and effects of digital accountability movements

Frequently Asked Questions

Get answers to common questions about cancel culture and its impact on society

What is cancel culture?

Cancel culture is defined as the environment wherein the act of boycotting or withdrawing support from a person or organization regarded as promoting socially unacceptable beliefs is considered commonplace and acceptable.

What are the causes of cancel culture?

Cancel culture is often caused by a mix of call for accountability and misplaced outrage. It usually begins when people online call out actions they find harmful, offensive, or unjust. In many cases, these reactions stem from valid reasons such as:

  • Actual crimes or proven illegal acts
  • Immoral but legal behavior, like exploitation, manipulation, or abuse of power
  • Actions that cause serious harm within a specific community
  • Hate speech or acts of discrimination based on race, gender, religion, or identity

However, cancel culture can also arise from less justified causes, where public emotion and misinformation take over. These include:

  • Personal dislike or hatred without solid reason
  • Ragebait or content designed to provoke outrage
  • Disliked media content such as social posts or videos
  • Bandwagon mentality where people join the "hate train" without knowing the full context

How does cancel culture take place?

Cancel culture occurs through public condemnation, mainly through social media platforms, to withdraw support from a person or organization that has acted objectionably.

What are the pros and cons of cancel culture?

Potential Benefits

  • Allows quick and easy justice when regular justice systems fail
  • Allows the marginalized to voice out their opinions on serious topics
  • Promotes freedom of speech and expression
  • Encourages responsible behavior
  • Democratizes consequences for wrongdoing

Potential Drawbacks

  • Creates bandwagon mentality, where people join in on the cancel without rationalizing the causes of the issue
  • Does not offer concrete solutions to resolve the issue being cancelled
  • No punishments are given to perpetrators of misdeeds as compared to regular justice systems
  • May cause offenses, primarily as hate crimes, worse than the original offense that was being cancelled

What are alternatives to cancel culture?

Instead of canceling others, we can choose approaches that promote understanding and growth. Educating people about the effects of their actions encourages reflection rather than hate.

We can also practice accountability with compassion by guiding others toward change instead of tearing them down. Creating respectful and supportive online spaces helps build a community that uplifts instead of cancels.

Steps You Can Take Today:

  • Think before you post or share
  • Respond to mistakes with empathy, not anger
  • Support open discussion instead of public shaming

Why It Matters:

These alternatives help us shift from canceling to connecting. When we focus on education, empathy, and accountability, we create an online culture that encourages growth, not fear. Real change begins when we choose empathy over anger and growth over destruction.

"Educate to empower, not to embarrass."

What do I do if I’m being cancelled?

Being cancelled can be overwhelming and deeply affect your mental and emotional well-being. Here's how to respond effectively:

Don't Act Impulsively

Pause and take a deep breath. Being cancelled isn't something most people experience, so it's normal to panic. However, reacting too quickly may worsen the situation.

Before responding, ask yourself:

  • What did I do that caused this situation?
  • Did my actions hurt anyone?
  • Are the accusations true?
  • What can I do to address this properly?

Assess the Situation Carefully

Understand what's really happening before you respond publicly. In cancel culture, there are usually two types of "cancelling":

  • Accountability-based cancelling: Where people call for justice and awareness over real harm done
  • Exaggerated or "hate train" cancelling: Where small mistakes are blown out of proportion by people who just want to join the outrage

Knowing the difference helps you respond wisely.

Take Action

If the accusations are true:
APOLOGIZE

Actions matter most, but a genuine apology restores dignity and rebuilds trust. Here's how to apologize effectively:

  • Take full responsibility for your actions. Don't make excuses or shift blame
  • Acknowledge how your actions affected others and show empathy
  • Avoid defensive statements like "Everyone makes mistakes" or "It wasn't my intention"
  • Put yourself in others' shoes and show understanding
  • Assure people you are willing to learn, grow, and change—and follow through
If the accusations are false:
  • Gather solid evidence to clear your name
  • Release a calm, factual, official public statement
  • In serious cases with real damage, consider legal action

Show Growth and Development

If accusations were true, apologizing is only the first step. People remember actions, not promises. Prove your sincerity by consistently demonstrating change. Your behavior and consistency rebuild your reputation—not your words.

Reflect

Being cancelled can be devastating, but it can also be a powerful moment for reflection. Ask yourself:

  • What are my core values and guiding principles?
  • How do my actions reflect what I stand for?
  • How can I use this experience to become better?

We all make mistakes, but what truly matters is how you handle them and what you learn from them.

Protect Your Sanity

In severe cases, it's crucial to protect your peace. Once you've taken responsibility or clarified your side, you don't owe strangers constant explanations.

  • Block or report toxic comments
  • Take a break from social media
  • Remember: in many cases, cancel culture is treated more like a "trend" than genuine justice
  • Surround yourself with supportive people who see your growth
  • Talk to a professional if needed

What do I do if someone is cancelling my loved ones?

Seeing someone you care about get “cancelled” can be painful and overwhelming. In times like this, your support can make a huge difference. Here’s how you can help:

  • Be a Source of Stability: Offer non-judgmental emotional support, listen without minimizing their pain, and remind them of their worth in this world.
  • Help Them Disconnect: Encourage them to step away from the internet for a bit, and help them set boundaries when they use it again.
  • Assist with Practicalities: Help them find legal or communications counsel, monitor the situation so they don't have to, and help them prioritize self-care.
  • Do Not Engage with the Mob: Defending them online can sometimes draw negative attention onto you or escalate the situation for your loved one. Support them privately.

Your calm presence, empathy, and consistency can be one of the few things that remind them they’re not alone through this experience.

When does freedom of speech go too far?

What is Freedom of Speech?

Freedom of speech is defined as a person's innate right to express themselves verbally. Since freedom of speech is innate, it is a human right.

However: Human rights also require the responsibility of respecting other people's rights.

How does it happen?

Freedom of speech goes too far when it violates another person's rights.

Cancel culture in particular, causes the loss of a person's dignity through cancelling, which is another human right.

What now?

Freedom of speech should be used to protect other people, instead of bringing them down.

If we do use our freedom of speech to condemn something, we should condemn the act, not the person.

In that way, we preserve the rights of other people, while still delivering proper justice to misdeeds.

Key Principle: True freedom of speech protects human dignity while holding actions accountable.

How to avoid cancel culture?

Being online can be a little scary. We're worried of saying or doing the wrong thing, and this generates anxiety over getting 'cancelled'. What if I say something people don't agree with? What if I make a mistake that I can't come back from?

Some people are already in that pit where they can't climb back up. They make decisions and act in ways that will define them for a long time. Whether you're worried about being cancelled or you're already in the pit of it, here are some tips for you:

Anxious Over Being Cancelled? Here's How to Avoid It:

Getting cancelled can feel scary, overwhelming, and sometimes threatening—but it's always something avoidable. Here are steps you can take to be more mindful of your online presence as suggested by MC Cayetano, a psychoeducational content associate at Mind You PH.

Don't Act Upon Your Emotions Immediately

Emotions are fleeting but strong—don't let them take hold of you, as you might end up saying something you'll regret later. Usually, one way we deal with strong emotions (usually negative ones) is through venting. However, research shows that this is actually unhelpful in the long run:

"...encouraging people to act out their anger makes them relive it in their bodies, strengthening the neural pathways for anger and making it easier to get angry the next time around. Studies on venting anger (without effective feedback) whether online or verbally, have also found it to be generally unhelpful."

— Suttie, 2021

So how should we cope with our emotions? Here are some helpful tips by Crystal Raypole:

Be Mindful of All Your Emotions

Accepting your emotions allows you to become more comfortable with them, and make you used to feeling them without getting overwhelmed (Raypole, 2024).

How to get more in-tune: A study from 2024 suggests mindfulness, meditation, and even psychotherapy can be helpful.

Keep a Mood Journal

Journaling can be helpful in reflecting back on your day—putting your thoughts on paper can help you trace emotions to events, reactions to triggers (Raypole 2024).

According to Reynolds (2025), some benefits include reduced stress, boosted well-being, and a better understanding of your needs.

Take a Deep Breath

Taking a deep breath can help ground you when you're about to get swept away in the moment. When you're beginning to feel overwhelmed, follow this simple breathing exercise:

1. Breathe in Slowly

Breathe in deeply, from your diaphragm.

2. Hold It

Hold your breath for three seconds. Become sensitive to the world around you: the sounds, the smells, the sensations. Try to imagine yourself in your happy place, where the world is calm.

3. Consider a Mantra

Some find an affirming mantra helpful. Examples: "I am calm." Or, "I am relaxed."

Stay on Top of Stress

Staying on top of stress can be tricky, even for those most in-tune with their personal well-being. Healthy ways to deal with stress include:

  • Getting enough sleep
  • Enjoy time with friends
  • Exercise
  • Spend time with nature
  • Make time for you and your hobbies

"It is very important to understand that emotional intelligence is not the opposite of intelligence, it is not the triumph of heart over head - it is the unique intersection of both."

— David Caruso

Practice Kindness

Remember the golden rule: do unto others as you would have them do unto you. A little bit of kindness can go a long way.

When you encounter someone who might frustrate you online, before retorting or reacting, think: is this a kind thing to say? Would I like to be treated this way?

Think Before You Click

This saying should be familiar to any online netizen: it's a reminder to be mindful of what we are saying. Before replying to someone online, make sure to apply the THINK standard:

T
Is it TRUE?

Is this fact or is it really an opinion or feeling? Know and be clear before you speak.

H
Is it HELPFUL?

Does it help you, them, or the situation?

I
Is it INSPIRING?

Also, does it IMPROVE on the silence?

N
Is it NECESSARY?

Would this better be left unsaid?

K
Is it KIND?

What is your motivation for communicating?

Detach From Social Media and Seek Real-life Experiences

When things might be getting a little too heated, when you get a little bit too absorbed with online discourse, put the phone down. Spend time with friends and family, reconnect with nature, or pick up a new hobby.

Here are some examples of things that you can do to keep yourself busy:

  • Find New Hobbies
  • Set Daily Goals or Write a To-Do List
  • Make Plans to See People in Real Life

Remember:

The internet can be as frightening as it is big. Just one wrong step can have you stuck in the same hole, forever. That's why it is important to always be mindful of our behavior and the way we present ourselves to others.

"You are free to choose, but you are not free from the consequences of your choice."
— Ezra Taft Benson
